RNAseq Analysis using GSE141910

Exploring gene expression differences in dilated cardiomyopathy (DCM) with a focus on sex-specific effects

1 Abstract

Dilated cardiomyopathy (DCM) is a major cause of heart failure characterized by ventricular dilation and impaired contractile function. Despite advances in clinical management, the molecular mechanisms underlying DCM remain incompletely understood, particularly with respect to sex-specific differences in disease progression. This study uses RNA sequencing (RNA-seq) data from the publicly available GSE141910 dataset to investigate gene expression differences between DCM and non-failing human heart tissue, with a specific focus on identifying sex-based transcriptional variation.

The dataset consists of 305 left ventricular samples, including 153 DCM and 152 non-failing controls, with representation from both male and female patients. Gene expression data were preprocessed through filtering and normalization steps before applying dimensionality reduction techniques such as Principal Component Analysis (PCA) and Uniform Manifold Approximation and Projection (UMAP) to assess global expression patterns. Differential gene expression analysis was performed using the limma package in R, followed by visualization through volcano plots. Additionally, KEGG pathway enrichment analysis was conducted to identify biological pathways associated with sex-specific differences in DCM.

Results show that disease status is the primary driver of gene expression variation, with DCM samples exhibiting distinct transcriptional profiles compared to controls. While sex-based differences were modest overall, they were more pronounced within the DCM group, suggesting that disease conditions may amplify underlying biological differences between males and females. Pathway enrichment analysis revealed significant involvement of inflammatory signaling, metabolic processes, and cardiac remodeling pathways, highlighting key mechanisms contributing to disease progression.

Overall, this study demonstrates that RNA-seq analysis can uncover both disease-specific and sex-specific transcriptional changes in DCM. These findings provide insight into the molecular basis of DCM and emphasize the importance of considering sex as a biological variable in cardiovascular research. Understanding these differences may contribute to more personalized therapeutic strategies and improved clinical outcomes for patients with DCM.

2 Introduction

Dilated cardiomyopathy (DCM) is a condition in which the heart becomes enlarged and weakened, reducing its ability to pump blood effectively. Over time, this impaired function can lead to heart failure, arrhythmias, and in severe cases, sudden cardiac death. DCM is one of the most common indications for heart transplantation worldwide and often affects individuals during early or middle adulthood, making it a major contributor to long-term cardiovascular morbidity (Halliday, 2019; Hershberger et al., 2013; McKenna et al., 2017; Pinto, 2016).

The causes of DCM are complex and involve a combination of genetic and environmental factors. A significant proportion of cases are linked to inherited mutations in genes that affect cardiac structure and function, particularly those involved in the cytoskeleton, sarcomere, and nuclear envelope (Hershberger et al., 2013). At the same time, non-genetic factors such as viral infections, toxins, and metabolic stress can also contribute to disease development. Despite these known associations, many cases remain idiopathic, highlighting gaps in understanding the underlying mechanisms driving disease progression (Halliday, 2019; Pinto, 2016).

Because of this complexity, studying DCM at the molecular level is essential for identifying the biological pathways that are disrupted in failing hearts. Transcriptomic approaches such as RNA sequencing (RNA-seq) allow for a comprehensive view of gene expression changes, providing insight into processes like inflammation, fibrosis, and altered metabolism that are often implicated in cardiac dysfunction (Dolce, 2019; Love, 2014; Wang, 2009). These large-scale analyses have become an important tool for uncovering patterns that are not visible through traditional clinical or histological approaches (Alimadadi, 2020; Margulies, 2016).

This project uses RNA-seq data to compare gene expression in DCM and non-failing heart tissue, with the goal of identifying key molecular differences associated with disease. In addition, this study explores whether these gene expression patterns differ between males and females. Sex-based differences in cardiovascular disease have been increasingly recognized, with evidence suggesting that biological sex can influence disease presentation, progression, and underlying molecular pathways (Kararigas, 2018; Raimondi, 2020; Regitz-Zagrosek, 2012). Understanding these differences may provide more targeted insights into DCM and support the development of more personalized approaches to treatment (Johnson, 2018).

3 Research Question

Which genes and molecular pathways differ between males and females with dilated cardiomyopathy (DCM) compared to non-failing hearts?

4 Hypothesis

DCM hearts show distinct gene expression and pathway changes compared to non-failing hearts, with stronger inflammatory and remodeling signals. These effects are expected to differ between males and females.

5 Discussion

This study demonstrates that dilated cardiomyopathy is associated with substantial changes in gene expression, with disease status acting as the dominant driver of transcriptional variation. This aligns with previous findings showing that DCM is characterized by widespread molecular remodeling involving inflammation, fibrosis, and altered metabolic activity.

While sex alone did not strongly separate samples in global analyses such as PCA, differences became more apparent within the DCM group. This suggests that disease conditions may amplify underlying biological differences between males and females. Prior studies have reported that sex hormones, immune responses, and metabolic regulation differ between males and females, which may contribute to variations in disease progression and severity.

The KEGG pathway analysis further supports this interpretation, highlighting pathways related to inflammation and metabolism. These findings are consistent with existing literature showing that chronic inflammation and metabolic dysregulation play central roles in heart failure and cardiomyopathy.

Importantly, this study adds to the growing body of evidence that sex-specific differences in cardiovascular disease are context-dependent and may only become apparent under pathological conditions. This reinforces the need to incorporate sex as a key variable in both research and clinical decision-making. Overall, the results provide new insight into how gene expression patterns differ in DCM and suggest that sex-specific mechanisms may contribute to disease progression through distinct biological pathways.

6 Limitations

This study has several limitations. First, the analysis was performed using bulk RNA-seq data, which does not capture cell-type-specific gene expression differences within the heart. As a result, observed changes may reflect shifts in cell composition rather than true transcriptional differences. Second, variability in patient characteristics such as age, comorbidities, and treatment history may introduce confounding effects that are not fully accounted for. Additionally, while statistical methods identify significant gene expression changes, they do not establish causality. Finally, the dataset includes an unequal distribution of male and female samples, which may influence the detection of sex-specific effects.

7 Future Work

Future studies should incorporate single-cell RNA sequencing to better understand cell-type-specific contributions to DCM and uncover more precise mechanisms driving disease progression. Expanding analyses to include additional datasets would improve generalizability and allow for validation of findings. Further investigation into specific genes identified in this study could provide insight into potential therapeutic targets. Additionally, integrating other data types such as proteomics or epigenomics may help build a more comprehensive understanding of DCM biology. Finally, exploring how sex-specific differences influence treatment response could contribute to the development of more personalized approaches to cardiovascular care.
